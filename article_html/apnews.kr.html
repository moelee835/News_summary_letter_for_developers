Becoming Art Through Mise-en-scène: Squarespace’s Super Bowl Campaign

[Editor’s Note] Korea AP-SinMun(News) Advertising Critique is a column featuring sharp, insightful analyses by a panel of experts in advertising, PR, and media. Recognized for its educational value and industry relevance, the series has been featured in South Korean high school textbooks and study guides.
※ Notice to Production Teams: If you find factual inaccuracies in this review or wish to present a counterargument, please email your comments to apnews@apnews.kr. We are committed to reviewing and reflecting valid feedback. Note: Phone inquiries are not accepted.

SNS 기사보내기

이 기사를 공유합니다

[KOREA AP-SinMun(News)  Advertising Critique No. 1493] Evaluation Period: February 20, 2026 - February 27, 2026

[AP신문 = 원다정 기자] The 1493rd Korea AP-SinMun(News) Advertising Critique reviews “Unavailable,” an ad released Feb. 4 by Squarespace, a U.S.-based website creation and hosting platform.

Emma Stone stars in the commercial, directed by Yorgos Lanthimos—the filmmaker behind her films The Favourite and Poor Things. 

Shot in black-and-white like a suspense thriller, it shows Stone on a remote island desperately trying to register emmastone.com, only to find it already taken, leaving her increasingly frustrated.

The ad builds tension through repeated failures, brisk pacing, and escalating humor, ultimately delivering the message that Squarespace helps users secure their own digital space and opportunities.

Aired during the 60th Super Bowl, it drew buzz and online virality from the star-director duo and striking mise-en-scène.

KOREA AP-SinMun(News) Advertising Critics' One-Line Reviews

Kwak Min-chol: The fear of domain preemption becomes art through mise-en-scène.

Kook Na-kyeong: Proving the value of presence through the discomfort of absence.

Kim Seok-yong: Bold and sensory, yet limited.

Lee Hyung-jin: Concise messaging and incredible immersion.

Jeon Hye-yeon: Instead of screaming, register first.

Hong San: Emma-Yorgos, once again!

Gemini: The brand’s visibility is blocked by artistic vanity.

ChatGPT: Artistic technique remains, but the brand-centered focus is lacking.

Advertising critics at Korea AP-SinMun(News) awarded the campaign a high score of 8.5 for Visual Aesthetics.

This was followed by Model Fit at 8.3, Clarity at 8.2, and Effectiveness at 8.0.

Other categories, including Creativity, Sound Design, and Likability, each earned a solid 7.7.

The campaign achieved an impressive overall score of 8.0.

Overwhelming Direction and Performance

Advertising critics at Korea AP-SinMun(News) praised the campaign for using black-and-white mise-en-scène and the conventions of the thriller genre to convey the urgency of securing a domain with the polish of an art film. They noted that Emma Stone’s frenzied emotional performance and the carefully constructed setting sustain tension and immersion until the very end.

It is a remarkable piece that channels the desperation of securing a domain through Emma Stone’s persona and stark black-and-white mise-en-scène. Most striking is how the direction expands the personal loss in the line “I lost my name” into a broader visual sense of dread, like being stranded on an isolated island. By stripping away color and relying solely on the contrast of light and shadow, the film captures Stone’s unhinged expressions and anguished cries, powerfully imprinting the message: “Claim it now, or lose it forever.” Rather than explaining the service, the ad boldly chooses to evoke emotion—an audacious strategy that reflects the brand’s confidence. Watching it feels like experiencing a short art film.

- Kwak Min-cheol, Critic (Score 9.0)

A well-crafted thriller distinguished by its striking visuals that hold the viewer’s attention until the very end. The monochrome palette, meticulous mise-en-scène, and classic thriller tone consistently build suspense. The peculiar house on a remote island and Emma Stone’s exaggerated screams sustain curiosity throughout. Meanwhile, the secretary—appearing with a laptop, internet access, and even inline skates—introduces an intriguing sense of dissonance that cleverly breaks genre conventions. The visual storytelling alone is strong enough to captivate the audience. The final line is also strikingly direct, clearly establishing the necessity of the brand and its role in resolving Stone’s predicament.

- Kim Seok-yong, Critic (Score 8.0)

The message of ‘securing identity in the digital world’ is delivered with striking clarity. By repeatedly showing a global superstar like Emma Stone frustrated by her inability to obtain a domain bearing her own name, the ad emphasizes that a domain is not merely an address but a valuable digital asset. The use of black-and-white cinematography creates a classic, dramatic atmosphere that contrasts effectively with the modern issue of digital identity, drawing viewers deeper into the narrative. Stone’s performance—portraying despair, anger, and frustration through repeated attempts and failures—further heightens the sense of immersion.

- Lee Hyung-jin, Critic (Score 8.0)

A Standout Problem-focused Messaging Strategy

Critics also highlighted the campaign’s bold narrative approach. Instead of following the conventional problem–solution structure, the commercial conveys why the brand is needed through the crisis itself.

Director Yorgos Lanthimos is one of Emma Stone’s closest collaborators in recent years. The ad’s visual direction and audio evoke the short film the two made together in Greece, as well as the opening of Poor Things. This ad also highlights a broader difference between American and Korean creative approaches: the former does not always need to show the exact moment when a solution resolves the problem. In this particular spot, Emma Stone realizes her desired domain is already taken—the problem—yet the commercial never actually shows her acquiring it through Squarespace, which would be the solution. It focuses exclusively on the problem while still communicating the brand’s purpose and value. This kind of creative approach is rare in Korea, where clients typically favor a complete story arc built around a predictable problem-to-solution sequence.

- Hong San, Critic (Score 7.6)

The commercial is a clever attempt to expand the simple message of domain registration using the conventions of a thriller. With Emma Stone at the center, the ad builds tension through repeated moments when a domain appears as “unavailable,” drawing viewers deeper into the narrative. The emotional outbursts that follow are exaggerated, yet that very exaggeration makes the message feel more immediate. Although the ad offers little direct explanation of the product, the psychology of FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) effectively fills that gap. The result carries the intensity of a short film crafted to deliver a single line of copy. Registering a domain may take just one click, but the moment you miss it becomes a thriller.

- Jeon Hye-yeon, Critic (Score 8.7)

Limited Intuition in Functional Communication

However, despite its strong cinematic quality and direction, some critics argue that the solution and functional message remain vague compared to the problem presented, weakening its persuasive power in conveying the brand’s practical value and driving consumer action.

It paradoxically underscores the need for building a website.  Emma Stone’s performance grounds the premise in a sense of realism and naturally carries the brand message forward.  Rather than listing the platform’s features directly, the ad highlights the value of the service through the inconvenience created by the absence of a website. Instead of enumerating product functions, it attempts to persuade viewers that the service represents “ownership of one’s digital identity.”

The film-like direction elevates the brand image, but it deliberately restrains detailed explanations of the product’s functions, choosing to leave an impression rather than deliver information.

- Kook Na-kyung, Critic (Score 6.7)

The strategic point is somewhat unclear compared to the visual polish. By repeatedly presenting the problem of “Unavailable,” the ad merely stimulates a sense of necessity without offering a satisfying solution, limiting its ability to prompt the target audience’s next action. It is also unclear whether the target volume and demand are large enough to justify a mass-market campaign.

- Kim Seok-yong, Critic (Score 8.0)

As expected from a Super Bowl–scale commercial pairing a film director and a major star, the visual execution is highly polished, but the way it ultimately connects to the product and brand message feels somewhat confined to artistic expression.  Because the link between the brand and the story is not entirely intuitive, its persuasive power with consumers may be weaker.

- ChatGPT, Critic (Score 7.1)

While the visual aesthetics and artistic achievement are impressive, the gap between the practical value of a website-building service and the ad’s metaphorical narrative feels too wide. The cinematic direction becomes so dominant that it obscures an intuitive understanding of what problem the tool actually solves, giving the impression that the ad leans more toward the creator’s self-indulgent artistry than broad public appeal. The lavish mise-en-scène ultimately overshadows the brand’s practicality, resulting in a commercial where style overtakes substance.

- Gemini, Critic (Score 6.7)
